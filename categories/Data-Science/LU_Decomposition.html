LU Decomposition

LU Decomposition is a widely used method of decomposing large systems of equations. It has common implementations and implications in data science and machine learning.

LU Decomposition involves taking a matrix and transforming it into the product of a lower and upper triangular matrix.

Example

The best way to understand LU Decomposition is to look at an example. Let's take the following matrix and decompose it.

A=[48−82−86−12−1810]A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 & -8\\ 2 & -8 & 6\\ -12 & -18 & 10 \end{bmatrix}A=⎣⎢⎡​42−12​8−8−18​−8610​⎦⎥⎤​

End Goal

We want to transform this into the product of two triangular matrices. The upper is commonly referred to as LLL, and the lower as UUU. The upper contains the diagonal that contains the 111s of the identity matrix, III.

In mathematical form, we want two matrices LLL and UUU such that

A=LUA = LUA=LU

where

L=[100c10cc1]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ c& 1 & 0\\ c& c& 1 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​1cc​01c​001​⎦⎥⎤​

and

U=[ccc0cc00c]U = \begin{bmatrix} c & c & c\\ 0 & c & c\\ 0 & 0 & c \end{bmatrix}U=⎣⎢⎡​c00​cc0​ccc​⎦⎥⎤​

where ccc, in both cases, are some arbitrary constants (ccc is not necessarily the same in those positions, it is solely a representation).

Methodology

One handy trick I learned along the way when practicing LU decomposition is to always keep track of the row operations! By doing that, we can build LLL by using the opposite of the row operations we used to build UUU.

If that is confusing, just wait. It will all make sense soon enough!

Obtain U

We need zeroes in the entire bottom row, and in the row 2, column 1 position. We use elementary row operations to do this.

R2=R2−12R1R_2 = R_2 - \frac{1}{2}R_1R2​=R2​−21​R1​
R3=R3+3R1R_3 = R_3 + 3R_1R3​=R3​+3R1​
R3=R3+12R2R_3 = R_3 + \frac{1}{2}R_2R3​=R3​+21​R2​
which would result in

U=[48−80−121000−9]U = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 & -8\\ 0 & -12 & 10\\ 0 & 0 & -9 \end{bmatrix}U=⎣⎢⎡​400​8−120​−810−9​⎦⎥⎤​

Now, we use the opposite of those row operations multipliers to build LLL.

Let's see what that looks like. Recall that LLL starts out as

L=[100101]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ & 1 & 0\\ & & 1 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​1​01​001​⎦⎥⎤​

Our first reversed row operation involves the multiplication of −12R1-\frac{1}{2}R_1−21​R1​, meaning that our first respective entry in the lower triangle will be the opposite of that multiplier coefficient.

L=[10012101]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ \frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ & & 1 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​121​​01​001​⎦⎥⎤​

Our next opposite multipliers are −3-3−3 and −12-\frac{1}{2}−21​. That leaves us with
L=[1001210−3−121]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ \frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -3 & -\frac{1}{2} & 1 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​121​−3​01−21​​001​⎦⎥⎤​

Results

A=[48−82−86−12−1810]=LU=[1001210−3−121]∗[48−80−121000−9]A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 & -8\\ 2 & -8 & 6\\ -12 & -18 & 10 \end{bmatrix} = LU = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ \frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -3 & -\frac{1}{2} & 1 \end{bmatrix} * \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 & -8\\ 0 & -12 & 10\\ 0 & 0 & -9 \end{bmatrix}A=⎣⎢⎡​42−12​8−8−18​−8610​⎦⎥⎤​=LU=⎣⎢⎡​121​−3​01−21​​001​⎦⎥⎤​∗⎣⎢⎡​400​8−120​−810−9​⎦⎥⎤​

More Applications

We can also use LU Decomposition to solve equations of the form

Ax⃗=b⃗A\vec{x} = \vec{b}Ax=b

Knowing that A=LUA = LUA=LU and substituting,

LUx⃗=b⃗LU\vec{x} = \vec{b}LUx=b

Then, we let Ux⃗U\vec{x}Ux equal some other variable, like YYY, and we're left with

LY=BLY = BLY=B
and
Ux⃗=YU\vec{x} = YUx=Y

Again, let's look at an example!

Example

Let's say we have the following system of equations.

A=[33−33−69693]∗[x1x2x3]=[12−1821]A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 & -3\\ 3 & -6 & 9\\ 6 & 9 & 3 \end{bmatrix} * \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12\\ -18\\21 \end{bmatrix}A=⎣⎢⎡​336​3−69​−393​⎦⎥⎤​∗⎣⎢⎡​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎥⎤​=⎣⎢⎡​12−1821​⎦⎥⎤​

We begin, again, with elementary row operations.

R2=R2−R1R_2 = R_2 - R_1R2​=R2​−R1​
R3=R3−2R1R_3 = R_3 - 2R_1R3​=R3​−2R1​
R3=R3+13R2R_3 = R_3 + \frac{1}{3}R_2R3​=R3​+31​R2​

Performing these to reduce AAA into proper UUU form, we are left with

U=[33−30−9120013]U = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 & -3\\ 0 & -9 & 12\\ 0 & 0 & 13 \end{bmatrix}U=⎣⎢⎡​300​3−90​−31213​⎦⎥⎤​

The opposites of the multipliers are effectively 1,21, 21,2 and −13-\frac{1}{3}−31​. Let's put those into the corresponding position of LLL

L=[1001102−131]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 2 & -\frac{1}{3} & 1 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​112​01−31​​001​⎦⎥⎤​

Let's recall the method we said we were going to use above: we're going to set up LY=BLY = BLY=B now.

L=[1001102−31]∗[y1y2y3]=[12−1821]L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 2 & -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} * \begin{bmatrix} y_1\\ y_2\\ y_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12\\ -18\\ 21 \end{bmatrix}L=⎣⎢⎡​112​01−3​001​⎦⎥⎤​∗⎣⎢⎡​y1​y2​y3​​⎦⎥⎤​=⎣⎢⎡​12−1821​⎦⎥⎤​

Notice that this is a fairly easy system to solve. This is why we love LU Decomposition! It happens to make it significantly easier for computers, too.

Thinking about this like a linear combination of vectors or an augmented matrix, we're left with the following solutions:

y1=12y2=−30y3=−13y_1 = 12 \newline y_2 = -30 \newline y_3 = -13y1​=12y2​=−30y3​=−13

Now, we must set up the equation Ux⃗=YU\vec{x} = YUx=Y

U=[33−30−9120013]∗[x1x2x3]=[12−30−13]U = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 & -3\\ 0 & -9 & 12\\ 0 & 0 & 13 \end{bmatrix} * \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12\\ -30\\ -13 \end{bmatrix}U=⎣⎢⎡​300​3−90​−31213​⎦⎥⎤​∗⎣⎢⎡​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎥⎤​=⎣⎢⎡​12−30−13​⎦⎥⎤​

Using back substitution to solve the system of equations, we end up with:

x1=3x2=6x3=−3x_1 = 3\newline x_2 = 6\newline x_3 = -3x1​=3x2​=6x3​=−3

And that's really all there is too it! We have solved the unique solution in this case, but this method works for no solution or general solutions as well.

Square Matrices and LU Decomposition

This brings us to the question: Do all square matrices even have an LU Decomposition?

This is an interesting question. The answer is mostly no, but there is a catch.

While there are counterexamples like

[1101]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}[10​11​]

which immediately disprove the idea, there are also examples where the specific matrix does not have an LU decomposition but does have what is called a PLU Decomposition, where the PPP stands for permuted. This is when rearranging the rows can permit for LU Decomposition.

To summarize, if you're working with large matrices, especially once you use computers for machine learning operations, LU Decomposition is an effective strategy if the conditions for it's usage are satisfied.
